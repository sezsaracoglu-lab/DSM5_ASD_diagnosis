⚠
Minimum for diagnosis: All 3 Domain A criteria plus at least 2 of 4 Domain B criteria plus Criteria C, D, and E — all must be present. Severity is rated separately for Domain A and Domain B (Levels 1–3).
A
Persistent deficits in social communication and social interaction
Across multiple contexts — not explained by general developmental delay
ALL 3 REQUIRED
A1
Deficits in social-emotional reciprocity
Abnormal social approach, failure of back-and-forth conversation, reduced sharing of interests or emotions
hook ▾
Exam hook
Reciprocity is the key word. Social interaction is not one-directional — it requires giving and receiving. A1 captures the failure of that fundamental exchange. In high-functioning ASD, the individual may want to interact but cannot sustain the back-and-forth rhythm of normal social conversation — they may monologue, talk at rather than with others, or fail to detect when a conversation has ended.

This is distinct from shyness or introversion: the quality of social engagement is impaired, not just the quantity. Link to ToM deficit: without automatic mentalising, the individual cannot track the other person's mental state and adjust their behaviour accordingly — making reciprocal exchange extremely difficult.
DSM-5 Examples
Abnormal social approach Failed back-and-forth conversation Reduced sharing of interests Failure to initiate / respond to social interaction Reduced emotional sharing
A2
Deficits in nonverbal communicative behaviours
Poorly integrated verbal/nonverbal comms, abnormal eye contact, deficits in gestures and facial expressions
hook ▾
Exam hook
Communication is more than words. A2 captures the nonverbal layer — the eye contact, body language, gestures, and facial expressions that neurotypical individuals use automatically and unconsciously. In ASD, verbal and nonverbal channels are poorly integrated: an individual may say appropriate words but with mismatched facial expression, or maintain eye contact without using it as an information channel.

EXAM TRAP Poor eye contact alone is not diagnostic — it must be understood in context. Some autistic individuals learn to make eye contact through camouflaging and therefore appear to pass this criterion on surface observation. Probe subjective experience: does the eye contact feel natural or is it a learned performance?
DSM-5 Examples
Poorly integrated verbal + nonverbal Abnormal eye contact Abnormal body language Deficits in understanding gestures Total absence of facial expression
A3
Deficits in developing, maintaining, and understanding relationships
Difficulties adjusting behaviour to social contexts, sharing imaginative play, making friends, absent interest in peers
hook ▾
Exam hook
Relationships require three distinct skills: (1) developing them — initiating appropriate contact; (2) maintaining them — sustaining them through reciprocal exchange over time; (3) understanding them — grasping the implicit rules and expectations of different relationship types. ASD can impair any or all three.

This criterion also captures the context-adjustment failure: neurotypical individuals automatically modulate their behaviour depending on whether they are talking to a boss, a close friend, or a stranger. In ASD, this social code-switching is effortful or absent. Note: A3 does not require absence of desire for friendship — many autistic individuals desperately want friends but cannot successfully form or maintain relationships. Desire ≠ ability.
DSM-5 Examples
Difficulties adjusting to context Difficulties sharing imaginative play Problems making friends Absent interest in peers
B
Restricted, repetitive patterns of behaviour, interests, or activities
Manifested by at least 2 of the following — currently or by history
≥ 2 OF 4
B1
Stereotyped or repetitive motor movements, use of objects, or speech
Simple motor stereotypies, lining up toys, echolalia, idiosyncratic phrases
hook ▾
Exam hook
Three modalities: body, objects, and speech. B1 spans stereotyped behaviour across all three. Motor stereotypies (hand-flapping, rocking, spinning) are the most visible. Object stereotypies (lining up toys rather than using them functionally) are diagnostically significant in young children. Speech stereotypies — particularly echolalia — involve immediate or delayed repetition of heard speech with no communicative function. DSM-5 explicitly includes speech stereotypies that were implicit in DSM-IV.

EXAM TRAP Echolalia can sound like communication — the child may repeat what they have heard in what appears to be a contextually relevant way. Always probe whether the speech is generated or reproduced.
DSM-5 Examples
Simple motor stereotypies Hand-flapping / rocking Lining up toys Echolalia Idiosyncratic phrases Pronoun reversal
B2
Insistence on sameness, inflexible adherence to routines, ritualised patterns
Extreme distress at small changes, difficulties with transitions, rigid thinking patterns, greeting rituals
hook ▾
Exam hook
Sameness provides predictability, and predictability provides safety. From a cognitive perspective, B2 reflects the inability to tolerate uncertainty. For an individual with ASD whose social world is confusing and unpredictable, the imposition of rigid routines creates a controllable, interpretable environment. Disruptions to routine are therefore not trivial upsets but genuine threats to an organising structure.

Link to Weak Central Coherence (WCC): detail-focused processing may increase sensitivity to small environmental changes that neurotypical individuals would not consciously register. Link to Executive Function deficits: difficulty shifting cognitive set makes flexible adaptation to change extremely costly.
DSM-5 Examples
Extreme distress at small changes Difficulties with transitions Rigid thinking patterns Greeting rituals Same route every day Same food
B3
Highly restricted, fixated interests — abnormal in intensity or focus
Strong attachment to unusual objects, excessively circumscribed or perseverative interests
hook ▾
Exam hook
The issue is intensity and focus, not topic. Many neurotypical individuals have special interests. In ASD, the interest is abnormal in intensity (consuming most of the individual's time, attention, and conversation) and/or abnormal in focus (the topic itself is unusual or the object of focus is atypical, such as drain covers, train timetables, or specific number sequences).

B3 is directly linked to Baron-Cohen's Systematising theory — the drive to systemise, analyse, and explore rule-based patterns. The restricted interest is often the domain the individual most excels at systemising. This is a genuine strength as well as a diagnostic criterion: savant-level abilities frequently emerge from the same focused processing style.
DSM-5 Examples
Strong attachment to unusual objects Excessively circumscribed interests Perseverative interests
B4
Hyper- or hyporeactivity to sensory input, or unusual sensory interests
Indifference to pain/temperature, adverse response to specific sounds or textures, excessive smelling/touching, visual fascination
hook ▾
Exam hook
NEW in DSM-5 — not present in DSM-IV. Sensory atypicalities were widely observed in clinical practice but were not formally included in the DSM-IV diagnostic criteria. DSM-5 explicitly incorporates sensory processing differences as part of the B domain.

The criterion is bidirectional: individuals can be hypersensitive (overwhelming sensory experience from ordinary stimuli — crowd noise, certain textures, bright light) or hyposensitive (apparent indifference to pain, temperature extremes, loud noises). Some individuals show both patterns simultaneously across different sensory modalities. Sensory overload is a significant contributor to behavioural meltdowns and may underlie apparent "B2" resistance to environmental change.
DSM-5 Examples
Indifference to pain / temperature Adverse response to sounds or textures Excessive smelling / touching of objects Visual fascination with lights or movement Sensory overload meltdowns
C·D·E
Additional required criteria
All three must be met alongside Domain A and Domain B
ALL REQUIRED
C
Symptoms present in early developmental period
May not fully manifest until social demands exceed limited capacities, or may be masked by learned strategies
hook ▾
Exam hook
"Early developmental period" ≠ "diagnosed in childhood." DSM-5 deliberately avoids specifying an exact age threshold (unlike DSM-IV's "before age 3"). The critical point is that symptoms must have been present — even if unrecognised — since early development. A high-functioning adult diagnosed at 30 still meets Criterion C if symptoms can be traced back to early childhood through retrospective history. EXAM TRAP Camouflaging and compensatory strategies can mask symptoms until social demands increase in adolescence or adulthood.
D
Clinically significant impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas
Symptoms cause real-world functional impairment — not sub-threshold autistic traits
hook ▾
Exam hook
The impairment threshold separates disorder from difference. The broader autism phenotype — autistic traits falling below diagnostic threshold — is present in the general population. Criterion D ensures that a diagnosis requires genuine functional impairment, not merely unusual or eccentric behaviour. Social impairment (difficulty forming/maintaining friendships), occupational impairment (inability to sustain employment), and daily living impairment all qualify. This criterion also links to the severity specifiers: Level 1 ASD still requires meaningful impairment.
E
Not better explained by intellectual disability or global developmental delay
Social communication below expected developmental level; ASD + ID comorbidity possible if social communication is disproportionately impaired
hook ▾
Exam hook
ASD and intellectual disability (ID) can and do co-occur. Criterion E does not exclude comorbid diagnoses — it requires that the social communication deficits are disproportionate relative to the individual's general developmental level. A child with ID may have delayed language as a consequence of general developmental delay; an autistic child with ID would show social communication impairments beyond what the ID alone would explain. Differential diagnosis requires developmental comparison: is the social deficit out of proportion to overall cognitive ability?
Severity specifiers — rated independently for Domain A and Domain B
LEVEL 1
"Requiring support"
Noticeable deficits without support; difficulty initiating social interactions; atypical responses; some success reducing RRBs with structured support.
Previously: Asperger's / high-functioning ASD
LEVEL 2
"Requiring substantial support"
Marked deficits; limited initiation; reduced or abnormal social responses; RRBs obvious and interfere with functioning across contexts.
Social + RRB impairments both apparent to casual observer
LEVEL 3
"Requiring very substantial support"
Severe deficits in verbal and nonverbal communication; very limited social initiation; minimal response to social overtures; extreme difficulty coping with change.
Previously: "classic autism" / Kanner's autism

Prevalence & Epidemiology

Prevalence: approximately 1 in 100 in the UK; higher rates (1 in 36) reported in some US CDC surveys — partly reflecting broadened diagnostic criteria.

Sex ratio: 4:1 male:female, though the female presentation is frequently subtler (camouflaging) leading to systematic underdiagnosis. The true ratio may be closer to 2:1 or 3:1.

Prevalence increase: multifactorial — broadened criteria, greater awareness, possible associative mating effect, and improved diagnostic tools rather than solely a true increase.

Interventions & Treatment
Davey 2021 · No cure exists — management targets behavioural symptoms and social functioning
5 APPROACHES
T1
Drug treatments
Haloperidol, risperidone, naltrexone — manage negative behavioural symptoms, not core ASD features
hook ▾
Exam hook
CRITICAL EXAM POINT No drug treats the core features of ASD. No pharmacological treatment currently addresses the fundamental social-communication deficits or restricted/repetitive behaviours. Drugs are used to manage negative behavioural symptoms in severe presentations — aggression, self-injury, hyperactivity — so that other interventions can be implemented.

Antipsychotics (haloperidol, risperidone) reduce repetitive/stereotyped behaviour, aggression, hyperactivity, temper tantrums, and self-abusive behaviour. Not all children respond; side effects include sedation, dizziness, significant weight gain, and movement disturbances (dyskinesias).

Naltrexone (opioid receptor antagonist) reduces hyperactivity and self-injurious behaviour. Symons, Thompson & Rodriguez (2004) found it decreased self-injurious behaviours by over 50% in 47% of participants. May also produce moderate increases in social interaction and communication.

The implication is always the same: drug treatment must be combined with behavioural, educational, and social interventions. Drugs alone cannot develop functional skills.
Key drugs & targets
Haloperidol → stereotypies, aggression Risperidone → hyperactivity, self-abuse Naltrexone → self-injury, hyperactivity Side effects: weight gain, sedation, dyskinesias
T2
Behavioural training methods (ABA / EIBI)
Operant conditioning to develop self-help, social, and communication skills; early intensive behavioural intervention is most evidence-based
hook ▾
Exam hook
The most evidence-based behavioural approach. Conditioning-based programmes use operant conditioning (reinforcement) to develop basic self-help, social, and communication skills. They also reduce disruptive behaviours (self-injury, aggression, repetitive behaviours) through functional analysis.

Key challenge: autistic children often do not respond to standard social reinforcers (praise). Clinicians use individualised reinforcers — pairing praise with tangible rewards to build secondary reinforcing value. Overselective attention means skills may not generalise; highly structured, repetitive trials are required to build each skill incrementally.

Evidence: Howlin (2005) — early intensive behavioural intervention is the most effective way of promoting long-term social functioning. Virués-Ortega (2010) meta-analysis showed significant gains in IQ, language, communication, socialisation, and daily living skills. Foundational figures: Davison (1964); Lovaas (1987).
Core targets
Eye contact (foundational skill) Toileting behaviour Peer interaction initiation Play behaviour Reducing self-injury via functional analysis
T3
Modelling
Demonstrating a required behaviour before prompting imitation — essential for non-verbal children with no existing communicative repertoire
hook ▾
Exam hook
Reinforcement alone cannot work without a baseline behaviour. Pure operant conditioning can only strengthen behaviours that already exist in the child's repertoire — you cannot reinforce what the child never does. For a non-verbal child with no existing communicative gesture, there is nothing to reinforce.

Modelling solves this by providing an external template: the therapist demonstrates the target behaviour (e.g., a Makaton sign) before prompting the child to imitate it. Only then can reinforcement be applied to strengthen the imitated behaviour.

Particularly effective for sign language and communication skill acquisition (Goldstein, 2002). Despite wide clinical use, relatively few properly controlled outcome studies exist — a limitation students should note.
Typical application
Teaching Makaton / sign language PECS (Picture Exchange Communication) Social script demonstrations Imitation → reinforcement sequence
T4
Parent-implemented early intervention
Parents trained to deliver behavioural techniques at home — more intensive, contextually relevant, and cost-effective than clinic-only treatment
hook ▾
Exam hook
The most efficient delivery model by evidence. Koegel et al. (1982) found that 25–30 hours of parent training was as effective as 200 hours of similar treatment by professionals in a clinic setting. Parents can also train other caregivers — school staff, siblings — broadening the intervention network at minimal cost.

Advantages: (1) learning occurs in the natural home environment where generalisation is most important; (2) intervention is far more intensive — parents are available far more hours than any therapist; (3) cost-efficiency; (4) benefits to parents themselves — McConachie & Diggle (2007) found improved maternal communication style and reduced maternal depression alongside child gains (Boone, 2018).

LIMITATION Requires significant parental time, energy, and willingness — resources not equally available across families.
Key evidence
Koegel et al. (1982): 25–30h parent training = 200h professional McConachie & Diggle (2007): child + maternal gains Boone (2018): maternal mental health benefits
T5
Inclusion strategies & supported employment
Community-based support for integration into mainstream education and employment — addresses both employee and employer sides
hook ▾
Exam hook
Individual skills training alone is insufficient. Even a high-functioning individual who has acquired relevant skills may still face significant barriers — job interviews, workplace social unpredictability, transition demands — that require ongoing support, not a one-time skills intervention.

Supported employment has four components: (a) employer training on managing an employee with autism; (b) employee job preparation and interview skills; (c) ongoing employee support for as long as needed; (d) regular feedback sessions with both parties.

Evidence: Mawhood & Howlin (1999) — supported employment participants showed higher employment rates and better social integration vs matched controls. Kilsby & Beyer (1996); Stevens & Martin (1999) — increased employee satisfaction and self-esteem.
Four components
Employer training & ongoing support Job preparation & interview scripts Ongoing employee support Regular feedback sessions

⚠ Treatment — Common Exam Traps

  • Drugs address negative symptoms, not core ASD. Haloperidol, risperidone, and naltrexone manage aggression, self-injury, and hyperactivity in severe cases. No current drug treatment addresses the core social-communication features of ASD.
  • Early intensive behavioural intervention is not a cure. It is the most evidence-based approach for promoting long-term social functioning, but outcomes vary considerably. Do not overstate effectiveness.
  • Modelling is a supplement to conditioning, not a replacement. It addresses the bootstrapping problem (no baseline behaviour to reinforce) but still relies on reinforcement to strengthen the imitated behaviour once elicited.
  • Parent-implemented intervention requires equal weight on its limitations. Koegel et al. (1982) evidence is striking (25–30h = 200h professional), but always note the limitation: unequal parental resource availability.
  • Supported employment targets both sides. A common error is describing supported employment as only providing the individual with skills. It also prepares and supports the employer — without this, individual skill gains have limited impact.
